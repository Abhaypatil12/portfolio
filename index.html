Projects

Embedded Smart Lighting Control System (Published Research Paper)

Developed an ESP32-based distributed smart street lighting system with grouped end nodes and a master controller for hierarchical brightness control and improved local sensing accuracy.

  • Problem: Traditional street lighting systems operate on fixed time-based switching — lights turn on at a set time in the evening and off in the morning. No intelligence, no adaptation, no feedback. The result is lights running at full brightness when it's still bright outside, or staying on during naturally well-lit nights — pure energy waste with no mechanism to detect failures or adjust to real-world conditions.
  • What I built: Distributed smart street lighting system on ESP32 — end nodes arranged in grouped clusters for localised LDR-based ambient sensing, coordinated by a central master controller in a hierarchical master-slave topology. Implemented PWM-based adaptive dimming driven by real-time sensor feedback, node-level fault detection with live alert reporting to master, and a Wi-Fi monitoring interface visualising per-node brightness, operational status, and system health across the entire network. Validated through prototype-level testing and experimental evaluation — published as a research paper in IJSREM.
  • Result: 40% reduction in energy consumption through adaptive ambient-based control. Full network visibility via centralised monitoring dashboard. Fault detection operational at individual node level with real-time master alerts.

  • Problem: Solar-powered systems in industrial environments have no intelligence about what's coming — they react to current power availability rather than anticipating it. When cloud cover drops generation unexpectedly, loads either get cut abruptly or batteries drain uncontrolled.

  • Result: Enabled predictive energy-aware load scheduling that reduced reactive load switching by ~35%, ensuring >95% stable execution of priority-based loads under variable solar generation conditions.
